As a first empirical sanity check, we implemented IEWC beside EF-EWC and EWC-DR in a small empirical package and ran synthetic continual-learning streams for classification, regression, diffusion denoising, and binary segmentation. These synthetic experiments used diagonal importances only to test whether the normalization mechanism behaves as predicted before larger benchmark runs; they are not the proposed practical approximation. The paper-facing real-data experiment below uses the low-rank IEWC approximation from Section 15. Here "full-data" refers to the use of the full benchmark split, not to a dense/full empirical-Fisher matrix.

Across three classification seeds, the sequential baseline had mean old-task accuracy drop $0.3568$, EF-EWC had $0.3190$, EWC-DR had $0.1029$, and diagonal IEWC with $\tau=0.001$ had $0.1673$. The EWC-DR result is strong on old-task retention in this toy classification setting, but it had lower new-task accuracy than the best IEWC setting. For diagonal IEWC, increasing $\tau$ from $0.001$ to $0.1$ increased old-task forgetting from $0.1673$ to $0.2559$ while improving new-task accuracy from $0.7962$ to $0.8952$, matching the expected stability-plasticity role of damping.

The same pattern appeared outside classification. In a phase-shift regression stream, mean old-task MSE increase over three seeds was $0.4106$ for the sequential baseline, $0.4028$ for EF-EWC, and $0.2617$ for diagonal IEWC with $\tau=0.001$. In a synthetic class-conditional diffusion denoising stream, mean old-task denoising MSE increase was $0.1102$ for the sequential baseline, $0.1086$ for EF-EWC, and $0.0856$ for diagonal IEWC with $\tau=0.001$. Both regression and diffusion runs showed the same tau tradeoff: lower $\tau$ preserved the old task more strongly and paid more on the new task.

The fourth task type, segmentation, was deliberately more severe: the two streams used conflicting pixel-intensity conventions. Here the sequential baseline and EF-EWC nearly fully forgot task A, with mean task-A IoU drops $0.9993$ and $0.9991$, respectively. Diagonal IEWC with $\tau=10^{-10}$ reduced the mean IoU drop to $0.9010$, but task-B IoU fell to $0.4168$. Larger $\tau$ restored task-B IoU and again forgot task A. This should be read as a stress test of the normalization mechanism and damping parameter, not as a final segmentation benchmark.

We also ran a full-data PermutedMNIST benchmark with 5 experiences, 10 epochs per experience, a 400-hidden-unit MLP, and a deterministic random 2,000-sample old-task sketch for low-rank importance estimation. Over three seeds, sequential training had final average accuracy $0.6906$ and average forgetting $0.3629$. Matched rank-20 low-rank EF-EWC with $\lambda=0.01$ had final average accuracy $0.8367$ and forgetting $0.1806$. Rank-20 low-rank IEWC with $\lambda=0.003$ and $\tau=0.001$ improved these to final average accuracy $0.8671$ and forgetting $0.1425$. The corresponding measured wall times were $126.8$ seconds for sequential training, $286.8$ seconds for low-rank EF-EWC, and $284.3$ seconds for low-rank IEWC; estimated FLOPs were about $1.00\times$, $2.63\times$, and $2.63\times$ the sequential baseline. A diagonal EF-EWC reference with $\lambda=100$ remains stronger on this benchmark, with final average accuracy $0.9347$ and forgetting $0.0442$, so the current empirical claim is not that low-rank IEWC dominates all EWC variants on PermutedMNIST. Rather, the result shows that the proposed low-rank IEWC approximation is usable at full benchmark scale and improves over the matched low-rank EF approximation.

We further tested very small ranks $k\in\{1,3,5\}$ and a low-rank-plus-diagonal variant, where the top-$k$ eigenspace is retained and the residual covariance is approximated by its diagonal. At fixed $k=3$, pure low-rank IEWC had final average accuracy $0.7877\pm0.0509$ and forgetting $0.2418\pm0.0635$, while low-rank-plus-diagonal IEWC improved these to $0.8162\pm0.0302$ and $0.2062\pm0.0374$. The measured wall time was $192.0\pm7.0$ seconds for pure low-rank $k=3$, $221.6\pm2.4$ seconds for low-rank-plus-diagonal $k=3$, and $127.9\pm1.6$ seconds for sequential training; estimated FLOPs were about $2.18\times$, $2.19\times$, and $1.00\times$ the sequential baseline, respectively. At rank 20, however, the same low-rank-plus-diagonal construction reached final average accuracy $0.8465$ and forgetting $0.1681$, below pure rank-20 low-rank IEWC while still above matched low-rank EF-EWC. Thus the residual diagonal is useful in the very-low-rank regime, although pure rank-20 low-rank IEWC remains the stronger current paper-facing benchmark.

The loss-scale measurements also support the mechanism. In the regression runs, the old-task IEWC loss-scale mean was $0.02125$ and the median was $0.01426$; in the diffusion denoising runs, the mean was $0.003394$ and the median was $0.002300$; in the segmentation runs, the mean was $3.36\cdot 10^{-9}$ and the median was $2.27\cdot10^{-11}$; in full-data low-rank PermutedMNIST, the final old-task IEWC loss-scale mean was $6.87\cdot10^{-4}$ and the median was $1.60\cdot10^{-10}$. A separate contamination diagnostic produced mean implicit EF sample weight $1.430236$ for contaminated examples versus $0.043146$ for clean examples, a ratio of $33.1486$. This is the behavior predicted by the contamination model: EF magnifies high-loss atypical samples, while IEWC normalizes away this loss-scale factor.

Finally, we tested the output-geometry claim with an ordered-output metric diagnostic. For adjacent shifts of probability mass on a one-dimensional output grid, a Wasserstein-style CDF metric gave mean loss $0.06658$ versus Euclidean mean loss $0.12475$, a ratio of $0.5337$. For far shifts, the same metric assigned higher loss than Euclidean, $0.33324$ versus $0.12484$. We then integrated the same geometry into the IEWC denominator as $G$ in a small ordered-output continual classification stream. With calibrated regularization strengths in a high task-B-accuracy regime, Euclidean IEWC had mean old-output Wasserstein drift $0.1766$, while Wasserstein-metric IEWC reduced it to $0.1649$; Euclidean old-output drift also fell from $0.2683$ to $0.2496$. This is preliminary but illustrates the intended role of $G$: IEWC can encode a local output geometry where nearby transport is cheaper than raw coordinate displacement, while long transport is penalized more.
